Cardiology

A cardiologist is a medical doctor who specializes in diagnosing, treating, and preventing disorders and diseases of the heart and cardiovascular system. They provide care for conditions like heart failure, coronary artery disease, and arrhythmias, and often work with patients to manage risk factors and develop treatment plans

A cardiothoracic surgeon is a medical specialist who performs surgical operations on the heart, lungs, and other thoracic organs. They treat conditions like heart disease, lung cancer, and thoracic injuries, often performing complex procedures such as heart transplants, bypass surgeries, and lung resections

A stress test, also known as an exercise stress test or treadmill test, measures how your heart functions during physical activity. You'll walk on a treadmill or ride a stationary bike while your heart rate, blood pressure, and electrocardiogram (ECG) are monitored. The test helps diagnose coronary artery disease, assess heart function, and determine your safe level of exercise

Some cardiopulmonary problems can have a hereditary component. Conditions like high blood pressure, heart disease, and certain lung diseases can run in families due to genetic factors. However, lifestyle and environmental factors also play a significant role. If you have a family history, talk to your doctor about your risk and potential preventive measures.

Most cardiac tests are non-invasive and painless. Some might cause minor discomfort. For example, a stress test might make you feel tired or short of breath. An angiogram might cause mild discomfort or bruising at the catheter site. Your doctor will explain the procedure and what to expect

There are many potential contributing factors, including incomplete medical history, misinterpretation of test results, lack of communication between healthcare providers, and complex or rare conditions. Research and efforts are ongoing to reduce diagnostic errors and improve patient safety.

Diagnostic errors are relatively common, affecting at least 5% of US adults, which translates to around 12 million adults every year. About half of these errors have the potential to lead to severe harm. Studies suggest that diagnostic errors occur in approximately 5.08% of outpatient cases, and diagnoses are wrong 10-15% of the time
